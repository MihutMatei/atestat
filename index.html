What is the British Parliamentary style of debate?

The British Parliamentary (BP) style of debate is a structured form of competitive debate that emphasizes the development of critical thinking, persuasive communication, and informed engagement with global issues.

What are the roots of BP debate?

The British Parliamentary (BP) style of debate traces its roots back to the traditions of the British Parliament, where formal debate has been a cornerstone of legislative procedure since the early days of British governance. The practice of structured debate in Parliament was designed to allow representatives to argue the merits and demerits of proposed legislation and policies, fostering a culture of rigorous public discourse.
The BP format as we know it today began to take shape in the 19th century, particularly in British universities. The Oxford and Cambridge Unions played a significant role in formalizing debate practices, setting the stage for what would become a globally recognized debating format. These university debates mirrored the adversarial style of parliamentary debates, emphasizing clarity of argument, persuasive speaking, and quick thinking.

How do you debate in this format?

The theoretical underpinnings of BP debate draw from classical and contemporary theories of rhetoric and argumentation. Classical rhetoric, as articulated by Aristotle, emphasizes the modes of persuasion: ethos (credibility), pathos (emotional appeal), and logos (logical reasoning).
The British Parliamentary (BP) style of debate is characterized by its unique structure, which involves four teams and eight speakers in each round. The teams are divided into two sides: the Government (or Proposition) and the Opposition. Each side consists of two teams: the Opening Government and Closing Government on one side, and the Opening Opposition and Closing Opposition on the other. This format encourages a dynamic and multi-faceted debate, as each team must not only present its case but also respond to the arguments of three other teams.

What are the speaker roles?

Opening Government (OG)

Prime Minister (PM)

First speaker for the Opening Government.

Responsible for defining the motion, presenting the case, and laying out the main arguments.

Deputy Prime Minister (DPM)

Second speaker for the Opening Government.

Supports the PM's arguments, introduces additional points, and refutes the Opposition's arguments.

Opening Opposition (OO)

Leader of the Opposition (LO)

First speaker for the Opening Opposition.

Responds to the PM's definition, presents the opposition case, and rebuts the Opening Government's arguments.

Deputy Leader of the Opposition (DLO)

Second speaker for the Opening Opposition.

Reinforces the LO's points, presents new arguments, and further refutes the Government's case.

Closing Government (CG)

Member of Government (MG)

First speaker for the Closing Government.

Extends the Government's case by introducing new arguments and perspectives.

Government Whip (GW)

Final speaker for the Closing Government.

Summarizes the Government's case, refutes the Opposition's new arguments, and provides a compelling conclusion.

Closing Opposition (CO)

Member of Opposition (MO)

First speaker for the Closing Opposition.

Extends the Opposition's case by adding new arguments and addressing the points made by the Closing Government.

Opposition Whip (OW)

Final speaker for the Closing Opposition.

Summarizes the Opposition's case, refutes the Government's new arguments, and concludes the debate effectively.
